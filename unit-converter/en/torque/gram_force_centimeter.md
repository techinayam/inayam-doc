## Understanding Gram Force Centimeter (gf·cm)

### Definition
The gram force centimeter (gf·cm) is a unit of torque that represents the rotational force applied at a distance of one centimeter from a pivot point. It is derived from the force exerted by one gram of mass under standard gravity (approximately 9.81 m/s²) acting at a distance of one centimeter from the axis of rotation. This unit is particularly useful in various engineering and physics applications where precise torque measurements are essential.

### Standardization
The gram force centimeter is part of the centimeter-gram-second (CGS) system of units. While the metric system has evolved, the CGS system remains relevant for certain scientific and engineering contexts. The standardization of this unit allows for consistent measurements across various applications, ensuring accuracy and reliability in calculations.

### History and Evolution
The concept of torque has been utilized since ancient times, but the formalization of units like the gram force centimeter emerged in the 19th century with the development of the CGS system. As engineering and scientific disciplines advanced, the need for precise torque measurements led to the adoption of various units, including the gram force centimeter, which remains in use today alongside the more commonly known Newton-meter (N·m) in the International System of Units (SI).

### Example Calculation
To illustrate how to calculate torque in gram force centimeters, consider a scenario where a force of 5 grams is applied at a distance of 10 centimeters from the pivot point. The torque can be calculated using the formula:

\[ \text{Torque (gf·cm)} = \text{Force (g)} \times \text{Distance (cm)} \]

In this case:

\[ \text{Torque} = 5 \, \text{g} \times 10 \, \text{cm} = 50 \, \text{gf·cm} \]
